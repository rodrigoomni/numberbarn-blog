How Long Does It Take To Port My Number?

Porting a phone number can feel like a mystery — will it take a day or a week? We break down exactly what happens behind the scenes and what you can do to speed things up.

Ella Delancey Jones
March 5, 2026  ·  5 min read  ·  Ask the Pig
Share

You've decided to switch — maybe you found a better rate, maybe NumberBarn is now holding your number, or maybe you're finally setting up that vanity number for your business. Either way, you need your number ported. The question everyone asks: how long is this actually going to take?

The honest answer: it depends. But we can give you a much better answer than that. Here's a breakdown of timelines by scenario, plus what you can do to keep things moving.

What Is Number Porting, Exactly?

Number porting is the process of transferring your phone number from one carrier (your current provider) to another (your new one). In the US, the FCC guarantees your right to keep your number when switching — this is called Local Number Portability (LNP). The new carrier initiates the request; the old carrier has to cooperate.

💡

Key fact: You must stay active with your current carrier until the port completes. Canceling your service early can result in your number being released into the wild — and losing it forever.

Typical Porting Timelines

Here's what to realistically expect depending on your situation:

  • Simple wireless-to-wireless port: 1–3 business days
  • Landline or VoIP port: 5–7 business days (sometimes up to 10)
  • Toll-free number port: 3–5 business days
  • Port to NumberBarn (parking): 3–7 business days on average
  • Complex or business accounts: up to 2–4 weeks

"The biggest delays we see aren't from the carriers — they're from customers who submit incorrect account info. Double-check everything before you submit."

The 5-Step Porting Process

Understanding each stage helps you know exactly where your port stands — and who to contact if it stalls.

  1. Submit a Port Request (LOA) You fill out a Letter of Authorization with your new carrier. This authorizes them to request the number from your current provider. Include your exact account number and billing address — small errors here cause 90% of delays.
  2. Validation & FOC Date Your new carrier submits the request to the Porting Center. If everything checks out, you receive a Firm Order Commitment (FOC) date — the official transfer date.
  3. Losing Carrier Review Your old carrier has up to one business day to reject the request (for errors) or accept it. Most acceptances are automated.
  4. Port Execution On your FOC date, the number transfers. For wireless numbers, this often happens within a specific two-hour window. Your old service drops, and your number is live on the new carrier.
  5. Confirmation You receive confirmation that the port is complete. Test the number — make a call or send an SMS to verify everything is working.

What Can Slow Down Your Port?

Most port delays are preventable. Here are the most common culprits:

  • Mismatched account name or billing address on the LOA
  • Incorrect account number or PIN
  • Number is on a business account (requires more documentation)
  • Number is flagged as part of a block or hunt group
  • Active promotions or contract locks with your current carrier
  • Submitting on a Friday afternoon (ports often don't execute on weekends)
